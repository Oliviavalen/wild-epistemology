It wants you to evolve, like a Pokémon. It’s telling you that it’s time to grow more, discover new horizons, unlock your wildest dreams because you know what? They’re not so wild. Maybe for the old you. But not for the evolved you.

Pikachu is cute and we all love his bond with Ash. But Pikachu and Ash are examples of willingly holding yourself back because you’re comfortable with where you are. It’s cute for a kids’ show, and as real people in the real world we tend to romanticize the quiet, cozy life, but you’re not meant to stay there forever.

Eventually, a desire for more will rise up from deep within—seemingly out of nowhere—and you’ll want more. “Deep within” is the poetic way of saying it’s your subconscious mind attempting to communicate to your conscious mind. That’s when you’ll know: it’s time for your adventure.

You can go willingly, like Bilbo, who pondered a little while before he set out on his quest for adventure. You can go reluctantly, like Luke Sykywalker who wanted to leave, but would never have allowed himself to go, so his entire life had to burned down to get him out the door. Or you can go through a traumatic event, like Bruce Wayne, whose parents had to be murdered to get him on his path.

You can go any which way you’d like, but you will go.

The Journey

But to chase the object of your desire would be to go on The Quest of Erebor just for the treasure inside Lonely Mountain. That’s not why Bilbo went. He went for adventure. The treasure, if he survived, was just a bonus.

If we don’t chase the treasure—the actual thing that we actually desire and probably the whole reason you’re reading this article—what do we chase?

We’re going to chase feelings.

What on earth does that mean?

I’m glad you asked. If you haven’t already read my Tortoise and Hare, what are you waiting for?



Every character and every element in every story—no matter what kind of story it is—is an ambassador, a personification, of your thoughts, feelings, and mental state. The entire story is about you.

Turns out, the world does revolve around you.

The comfortable hobbit living a small life, the dragon hoarding his treasure, the evil wizard trying to kill you, the great storm… it’s all you, babe. Should I write a whole article on this? I can.

Everybody knows it’s not the destination; it’s the journey. Right? Of course, right.

So instead of chasing the treasure—the thing we want—we’re going to just follow our feelings. What feeling are we going to follow?

Good question.

We’re going to chase the feeling of adventure.

You don’t have to be an empath to know what adventure feels like. How has adventure felt for you in the past? When you built your business, found twue wuv, or mastered a skill you were passionate about?

You say you haven’t had any adventures of your own? I doubt it. But that’s okay.

How does adventure feel in your favorite books and movies?

Adventure starts with curiosity. What if…

What if I go on this adventure? What if I investigate that smelly cave? What if I put on this ring? The fact that it’s evil is beside the point. Look at how much it helped him. Look how cool it was when he put it on. He could turn invisible, hear the spiders talking, save his friends, and have his hero moments. Multiple times. Like, wow!

Adventures feel exciting and thrilling. Look at Bilbo leaving the Shire and going out into Middle Earth for the first time when he’s no bigger than a man’s knee. How exciting for him! We’re cheering him on every step of the way.

Adventures feel perilous but in a good way. We know the dangers ahead. He’s going to fight orcs, and almost get eaten by trolls and spiders, but he’s going to make it because he’s the main character. Even though he’s severely unqualified and was only brought along so the fellowship wouldn’t be an unlucky 13. But that matters not!

Adventures feel rewarding. We know all the treasure he’s going to get. A magic ring, an ancient weapon forged by immortals, a chest of treasure, lasting friendships, his own campfire tales, and the enduring satisfaction that he freakin’ did it. I mean, it doesn’t get more rewarding than that.

Adventures make you feel alive. You may never know where you’re going to get your next meal or have a solid night’s sleep but that’s what makes it exciting. That anticipation is what makes you feel alive.

Adventure challenges your mind. You have use your wit and critical thinking skills on-the-go. No time to sit down and plan it all out. You’ll be dead. Learn and go.

Those are the feelings we’re going to follow.



Every day when you wake up, you’re going to do the things that make you feel the way your favorite characters feel when they go on adventures.

That’s how you manifest your desires, get your prayers answered, consistently make miracles happen, whatever. It’s full-proof.





The Formula

For me to get what I want, and for you to get what you want, we have to know how to use this information to actually help us. By using the story as a guide, we now know a few things about manifesting our goals/shaping our realities:





Desire is a quest, not an object. We don’t visualize quests. We embark upon them.



The quest takes place in our heads. (Actions in the real world naturally proceed thoughts.)



The quest is following feelings. We’re going to do things that make us feel the feelings we’ve decided to follow.



The feelings we follow are curiosity, excitement, thrills, perilous fright (the things that scare us but lead to growth), and aliveness. 



The Application

That said, when you want to manifest something, do this:





Identify what you want



Think about all the times you got something or did something impossible. Remind yourself how you just knew it would happen and it did. No effort required.



Now let it go.



Emergency protocol: if the idea of it pops up into your head again just quickly remind yourself of all the impossibles you’ve achieved already and let that be your trophy on your mental wall. Every time you catch yourself wanting money or a kindred friend, you’re going to trade that thought with the thought that reminds you of what you’ve already accomplished. So instead of obsessing over not having something, you’re going to obsess over reliving your glory days.

Don’t worry—there’s more. I’m not leaving you hanging there. However, this is your new foundation and we need to make sure the concrete sets right before we throw up some 2x4s.

The Foundation





Nature despises a vacuum. You can’t just get rid of a thought that constantly plagues you. You have to fill the void with another thought takes up as much room as the old thought. If you don’t that old one is going to come back with friends—you’ll be second-guessing yourself 10x more than you ever thought possible. This is why the Bible says to fill the house with pure spirits after you evict the old one.



Imagine a pendulum swinging really far to the left, then equally to the right. This is what we’re doing with your belief of lack. First it will mellow out, then it will stop. But only if you replace it with another equal obsession that works in your favor. That part is imperative.



Do not force yourself to feel “positive” vibes, intense excitement, or any of that toxic positivity culture. Just calmly remember your #4 and go about your day. When you feel the good feels, it will be organic and therefore sustainable. Don’t force it. 



You can’t turn lead into gold instantly. You must transform it. You’re transforming your old thought to a new thought. The good news is, transformation is a skill. You can do it so fast that it looks like you skipped 50 steps when all you really did was go through them expeditiously. Like any other skill for any other discipline, practice, consistency, and inner resolve are key. It can take you 5 seconds, 5 days, or 5 years.



Don’t let anybody else’s results get into your head. In fact, mind your business. Don’t look up manifestation techniques hoping you’ll expedite the process. You’ll get overwhelmed, start second-guessing yourself, get mad you failed, and feel like you have to start over once you’ve had time to mentally recover.

There are No Negative Feelings.



In a lesser-known quote by Marcus Aurelius, he defines good as what’s good for him, and bad as what’s bad for him. Good is what’s good for you. Bad is what’s bad for you.

You may be someone who runs away from those so-labeled negative vibes so much that your body is in desperate need to open the flood gates and let them all out. On the other hand, maybe you’re so overwhelmed with grief and anxiety that your body needs you turn off the faucet for a while and make room for  laughter. It’s okay to feel sad, but it’s also okay to feel happy.

Good emotions are the ones you need to express. Bad emotions are the ones you’ve allowed yourself to get stuck in, and are now holding you back.

You went to sleep last night worried about bills or tired of being alone. You woke up today, and those feelings were still there. You still had mouths to feed, dishes to wash, a job to work, an empty home, a purposeless life, whatever it is that’s causing you to feel this way.

That’s okay. That’s life. 

For most people, simply ignoring “negative” feelings is ineffective, unsustainable, and ends up causing more harm than good.

First, you try to ignore them, then problems escalate until you lose your mind. Then you feel even worse because you failed at trying to be better and now you realize you’re never going to get better so why even try? Then you retreat to your home and stay there until you have enough energy to try again. It’s a vicious cycle that goes on for years, decades, generations, and entire lifetimes. 

You won’t get anywhere just by ignoring them. You shouldn’t ignore them. Without those feelings, you wouldn’t be motivated to want more. Desire needs absence to grow. Take a moment to embrace them. 

Embrace the depression, the anxiety, the fear, the doubt, the feeling of inadequacies, the loneliness, the isolation, the misery. All of those emotions are proof that your inner fire, your inner Calcifer, is still alive enough to know that it wants. Embrace them.

Just a quick nod though, don’t build a shrine.
